Evidence of Teaching Efficacy

Here I have curated feedback on my teaching from several sources. Student responses include anonymous opinions from end-of-semester evaluations, as well as comments on my teaching or the project made in graded final project reflections. Also included are thoughts from the lead professor for The Art of Engineering, and excerpts from a peer observation report prepared as part of our work in the Teaching Observation Fellowship. Stay tuned for quantitative evidence. I have organized the feedback into topics, which touch on some of the major themes of my teaching statement.

Unless otherwise noted, all feedback refers to my work from Fall 2019 to Spring 2023 on the computer engineering project for first-year undergraduates. Participation in this project varied from 10-60 students, depending on the semester.

General Comments

"One of my favorite TAs I've ever had!"

"Very knowledgeable and passionate teacher, inspired in me an admiration for microprocessor design."

"Always available to explain the material and honest with the workload."

"Caleb's lectures and assignments taught me everything I wanted to know about computer engineering and nothing I didn't need to. He explained the computer engineering design project using clear and concise language. I hope that when I grow up I'll be as cool as him."

Quotes taken from end-of-semester evaluations, including one from my time as a teaching assistant in the third-year circuit analysis lab.

Course Structure

"I found myself really enjoying the structure of the class, with the biweekly schedule allowing me plenty of time to understand this very new material."

"​​I enjoyed the modules prior to the lesson which allowed me to understand the information at my own pace. The notes were thorough and easy to understand."

Student excerpts taken from their final assignment reflections.

The lead professor remarked that my project was "absolutely outstanding in all aspects - technical content, organization, and communication."

My peer teaching observation fellow commented that my "materials and presentation were well-prepared and mindful of different learning preferences and paces."

Feedback from faculty and graduate student peer both from the first iteration of the project, in Fall 2019.

Receptiveness to Student Needs

"He always went above and beyond to make sure we were truly learning the material. He even had a survey early on in the project and restructured part of his teaching to accommodate what was said in the student surveys."

My peer observer remarked that "Caleb showed an eager responsiveness to student questions and always used students’ input to build and deepen further understanding around a topic...His explanations and responses to the questions that students posed showed his knowledge of the subject and his commitment to effectively communicating it to students just entering the field of computer engineering."

From end-of-semester evaluation.

Impact on Student Educational Plans

"This project made me actually excited to major in computer engineering!"

"I don’t believe that more computer engineering lies in my educational future. That said, I feel that I am a better computer scientist for knowing exactly how our computers are constructed and the limitations that they impose."

"I am really glad to have chosen this department project. As a prospective computer science major, I have to admit I was a little nervous after the first class. This would push me out of my comfort zone, and force me to understand the computer beneath the code…I will definitely look into taking a full class on computer engineering in the future, as this department project was extremely enjoyable. Thank you!"

"I have never liked the idea of building processors, circuits, logic and all that, but the amazing experience of working on this project has made me seriously consider studying more about this stuff in college. That would never have happened if I didn’t step out of my comfort zone to try something new by signing up for this project. Thank you for your time, commitment and excellent teaching! This project has and will have a lasting effect on me. I’m extremely grateful and keep up the good work."

Since there is no separate computer science project in The Art of Engineering, a common challenge I face involves engaging students who are only interested in software, and might find the processor design project lacking in relevance. Fortunately, I frequently see comments in students' final reflections such as those included here, in which software-oriented students acknowledge the value of the more hardware-focused project.
