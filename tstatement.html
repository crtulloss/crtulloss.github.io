Introduction: Lab-Centered Instruction

Whether it is in academia or industry, in computer simulations or at a physical bench, electronics engineering is centered around lab work. Thus, while undergraduate students (even in engineering) might expect to sit passively in a lecture, when they enter my classroom, they are learning to live the lab experience. This is not to say my only teaching experience is in lab courses, although I have served as a TA in such settings. Rather, I view every component of instruction as an opportunity for lab activity.

Why? Because the lab is the setting that best captures the key learning goals of engineering as a whole: students must use their theoretical knowledge to build a solution to an engineering problem, then characterize their solution using appropriate measurements and communicate the results to other engineers. Explicit in this process is the requirement that students produce something, which is a more difficult and more meaningful task than the passive understanding and identification of concepts associated with a lecture.
